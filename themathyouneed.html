Intro

Here we learn algebra sophistication, problem solving strategies, matrix calculus, integration for asymptotic analysis and the Lean proof assistant combined with a nice logic method called natural deduction so we can verify our own proofs are correct.

Since we need an actual useful integration technique for applications we will learn the modern version of the Newton integral instead of the Riemann integral with the added benefit of the Newton integral being a good foundation to proceed to other integration methods if you want. Undergrads take multiple semesters of the Riemann integral in the introductory calculus sequence then again in a real analysis course but graduate instruction promptly abandons that theory completely for the Lebesgue integral.

Prove the laws of multiplication

We'll do this in the Lean proof assistant too.

Prove n * 1 = n

The definition of multiplication: mn is n + n repeated m times so n1 is 1 + 1 repeated n times so 11 + 12 + … + 1n = n

We will need this result for the other proofs.

Prove the distributive law: (k + n)m = km + nm

km is m + m repeated k times or m1 + … + mk and (k + n)m means m + m repeated (k + n) times. Distributing m in (k + n)m we get: ((m1 + m +…mk) + (m1 + m +…mn)) and now the left side matches the right side since km + nm is the same when expanded.

Prove the associative law: (kn)m = k(nm)

(kn)m is m + m repeated kn times and kn is n + n repeated k times:

(n1 + … + nk)m or (mn1 + … + mnk) using distributive law.

Right hand side: k(nm) is nm + nm repeated k times (nm1 + … + nmk) and factor out m, both sides of the equation are the same.

Prove the commutative law: nm = mn

First let's prove n1 = 1n or 1n = n. We proved n1 = n already, so we can use substitution to replace n in 1n.

1n is 1(11 + .. + 1n) and using the distributive law this is (11 + .. + 1n) or n. So now we have n1 = 1n.

Looking at the right side: mn is (n1 + … + nm)

Factor out n: n(11 + .. + 1m) and a sum of 1's up to m is m, so we have nm = nm.

Derivatives

A derivative of a function is another function you derive from the original function. Calculus is really only about derivatives as integration is an antiderivative procedure meaning you can get back to the original function (sometimes a set of functions) from it's derivative by integration hence it's name to integrate.

GeoGebra examples

Try moving the sliders around in this GeoGebra example. The slope can be interpreted as the sensitivity of a function, if the slope is very large for small x inputs then y is sensitive to changes in x. You find the slope of a function by taking the ratio of the distance between two points. This is \(\Delta\) 'delta' the change between two points:

\(\begin{equation*} m = \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{y_{2}-y_{1}}{x_{2}-x_{1}}\text{.} \end{equation*}\)

In this example move the points a and a+h along the x-axis, a represents some fixed input and h is a tiny displacement added to f(a) for example if this function is f(x) then f(2) is a the specific input, and f(2 + 0.1) is what a+h represents. \(\Delta x\) = h and \(\Delta y = f(a + h) - f(a)\) because the outputs of f(x) are plotted y. The 2 points (a, f(a)) and (a+h, f(a+h)) give us a secant (Latin for cut) a line that intersects at 2 points. The slope of the secant is the average rate of change:

\(\begin{equation*} m_{sec} = \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}= \dfrac{f(a + h)-f(a)}{h}\end{equation*}\)

In the same example, the tangent (Latin for touch) seems to only touch our function at one point but we need two points to find it's slope. We can approximate the slope of the tangent by imagining some process where the a and a+h or \(\Delta x\) interval shrinks until their difference approaches zero but doesn't reach zero. We then take the limit of this process and if the limit exists then we have an approximation of the tangent slope which represents the instantaneous rate of change at point a or the derivative of a.

The most intuitive explanation I've seen of instantaneous rate of change is given in the MIT OpenCourseWare series Calculus Revisited where in the course introduction video he explains how differential calculus is the study of the ratio of zero/zero so a limiting process is introduced using the example of two observers who approach each other but never touch so the distance between them is never actually zero it's an infinite process where you can always cram in smaller intervals such as 1/million or 1/billion to get closer approximations. The observers are so close the car speeding past them can be considered standing still as it's a snapshot in time of the instantaneous speed, or as he points out a ratio over (almost) 0.

\(\begin{equation*} m_{tan} = \lim_{h \to 0}\dfrac{f(a + h)-f(a)}{h}\end{equation*}\)

Here we see if you take the tangent of all points of the black function, it is specifying another function in grey. We want to work at the higher-order functions level and have the function that gives a derivative at a specific point instead consume entire functions as input and return new functions that are the derivative. We accomplish this by adjusting the notation to show entire functions are being input:

\(\begin{equation*} f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{f(x + h)-f(x)}{h}\end{equation*}\)

Derivative example 1

Let's walk through inputting f(x) = x2 into the derivative function:

\(f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{f(x + h)-f(x)}{h}\)

x2 is (input)2 our derivative function is f(input + input) - f(input):

\(= \lim_{h \to 0}\dfrac{(x + h)^2 - x^2}{h}\)

Expand using binomial theorem

\(= \lim_{h \to 0}\dfrac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h}\)

Since h is 'approaching zero' h2 is an even smaller value we can ignore so either treat it like an asymptotic or substitute h2 for 0:

\(= \lim_{h \to 0}\dfrac{2xh + \epsilon^{O(1)}}{h}\) or \(\dfrac{2xh + 0}{h}\)

\(f'(x) = 2x\)

If you go back and do it again with f(x) = 2x you would get the second derivative f''(x) = 2, a function that returns a constant. These three functions are all related as we'll see shortly in Interpreting the derivative.

\(= \lim_{h \to 0}\dfrac{2(x + h) - 2x}{h}\)

Factor out the 2

\(= \lim_{h \to 0}2 \cdot \dfrac{x + h - x}{h}\)

\(= \lim_{h \to 0}2 \cdot 1\)

\(f''(x) = 2\)

Try it with xn the output is nxn-1 which is the general formula for taking the derivative of any polynomial/power function where (x + h)n expands to xn + nhxn-1 + hO(1) and all those h powers you discard.

Derivative example 2

Let's try finding the derivative of f(x) = 1/x

\(\lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{\dfrac{1}{(x + h)} - \dfrac{1}{x}}{h}\)

Factor out 1/h and do the subtraction in the numerator:

\(\lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{1}{h} \cdot \dfrac{\dfrac{x - (x + h)}{x(x + h)}}{1}\)

This was in the laws of division video \(\dfrac{k/m}{n}\) = \(\dfrac{k}{mn}\):

\(\lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{1}{h} \cdot \dfrac{x - (x + h)}{x(x + h)}\)

Distribute negative sign and simplify some more:

\(\lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{1}{h} \cdot \dfrac{-h}{x(x + h)}\)

Distribute back in 1/h to cancel h:

\(\lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{-1}{x(x + h)}\)

Take the limit h -> 0 substituting h = 0

f'(x) = -1/x2

Descartes on why you should learn math

Leibniz's 'calculus of thought' or precise rules of mental reasoning can also be found in a different form in the preface of Descartes Discours de la Methode. Every modern copy extracts the preface of the book, treating it as the actual book removing his geometry, optics and metereology work which we will shortly see are mandatory for understanding Descartes method. The preface is 6 parts hastily slapped together by Descartes because he was pressed for time where only Part Six, One and Two are the most interesting the rest seem totally random like a long discussion on blood circulation and metaphysics. The best translation is by Ian Maclean for Oxford's World Classics and can be found on library genesis where every translation from 1600s French colloqialisms has an accompanying commentary at the back of the book. The famous quote in this book 'I think, therefore I am' was written as a refutation to Aristotle and his syllogisms as an example that is logically faulty but somehow this quote circulates without pointing out it's context.

However not included in that book is the chapters on geometry, and all the correspondence Descartes wrote after he was 'attacked in pamphlets being circulated'. Descartes hated reading long books, and hated school. This is why he routinely writes don't worry reader, the following isn't too long in many of his chapters hoping to convince the reader to actually read it. His opinion was the best method of mental reasoning was in the geometry texts of Archimedes, Pappus, Apollonius 'and others', because they produced self-evident arguments 'not quite easily known' that don't exactly follow a straight line of deduction. He explains Archimedes comparison of the sphere to a polyhedron in order to learn more about the sphere 'directs the light of nature in such a way that we pay particular attention' and that even if the reader denied the results, they could be pointed to similar results from previous proofs to show it must be true. In other words what he means by this is discoveries made this way aren't always direct consequences of previous deductions where the geometer follows A to B, but independent discoveries done with analysis that once found can always be proven in the framework of geometry by pointing back to some previous proofs as a source of truth. He preferred this kind of demonstratable proof that couldn't be refuted compared to philosophy which considered every possible sides of an argument and produced no truth.

He then laments that almost nobody reads those texts, because they are lengthy and require special attentiveness so are only known by a handful of people. He also laments that discoveries aren't made in formal geometry they're made in analysis (Pappus' projective geometry) so the reader doesn't get to see how the discovery was made only the final results, which do not proceed from A to B, and this is why he wrote his appendix on cartesian geometry the whole point was to create a new analysis that could be easily learned and demonstrate this way of thinking of 'discovering the truth'. He was especially thrilled how short it was. The others on optics and metereology were again simply used as examples how you would go about using his method to make discoveries, and connect directly to the preface about his method yet these are all omitted today.

This is why Isaac Newton attacked Descartes and spent the rest of life trying to refute his philosophy on only learning analysis, because he claimed the analysis wasn't important at all, and that anybody could have figured it out independently just screwing around in their spare time like he did, and that's why the ancients had largely hidden it preferring to show to final proofs in other words no results in math could exist without synthesis meaning demonstrate a proof. Maybe he's right considering the analysis he used of infinitesimals during that time was unproven hacks he did for self-entertainment while on plague lockdown but when it came time to write the Principia he had to prove the analysis, which nobody else could, so performed synthesis showing a limit can exist in the framework of geometry.

Much like Descartes I too don't like watching long lectures, reading long books, so have tried to create this workshop to be as brief as possible highlighting things I've found interesting where we aren't doing the entire Proof and Logic book, and we aren't doing the measure theory section of The Calculus Integral we're just getting enough math so that we learn this style of thinking and can self-direct our own research according to interest.
