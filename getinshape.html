Heavy metals, pesticides

I was surprised to learn that there is varying levels of arsenic, lead, mercury, cadmium and of course pesticides in spinach, carrots, all fruit, basically anything you eat has some kind of heavy metals in it and if it's a plant likely pesticides too. Taking the average of all recent papers, any leafy greens in particular spinach had one of the highest levels of arsenic, cadmium and lead. Fish that consume other fish had the highest levels of heavy metals where sardines had less heavy metals due to them being on the lower end of the ocean food chain. A few 2021 and older studies found that washing leafy green vegetables with basified water with sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) removed almost all of the pesticides and significantly decreased the metals leading them to conjecture that heavy metals contamination was mainly 'foliar deposition' which I assume means aerosol surface contamination of the leaves ie: you can wash it off. As a contradiction microbiome researchers advocate to wash food less so you get increased beneficial bacteria in your gut.

I didn't have the time to categorize, sort and average pet food contamination but my scrape found many recent pet food testing studies on heavy metals content in dry pelleted food either plant or meat based, many countries have no specific legislation guaranteeing the content.

In other research, on average microplastics have been found everywhere including freshwater samples in the highest elevation lake in North America leading to the conjecture that microplastic dust exists in our atmosphere and contaminates everything but this was described as 'potentially a problem', where microplastics in seafood had an effect on their health it is still inconclusive it's effect on humans who pass them when consumed however the primary concern of breathing and injesting microplastics is the fact they pick up nasty toxins in our environment that cling to them, not the injestion of microplastics themselves yet anyway.

Intermittent fasting

Intermittent fasting or IF is a catch all term for numerous different fasting protocols such as feeding time windows like only consume food during a 8-12hr period in 24hrs, only eat during sunlight (circadian rhythm diet) or 5:2 diet, 5 days feeding and 2 days fasting type schemes. In animal models there is many touted benefits but does this translate to humans?

These guys analyzed all non-animal model time restricted eating studies from 2000 until 2019. Result: inconclusive, and with the highest dropout rates of participants meaning odds are if you try this yourself, you're going to quit. "Intermittent fasting has gained much attention as a popular weight-loss strategy over the last few years. Controlled studies demonstrated that IF (which can have very different protocols) is a safe alternative to daily caloric restriction, with similar weight loss results. Nonetheless, its effects on weight are directly related to caloric restriction and not the metabolic effects of fasting". In other words, under the calories in/calories out model of weight maintenance, IF may help with eating less calories since you can't graze on food all day and have to observe a window of eating time but all it's beneficial effects in animals do not manifest in humans.

Taking the average of all 2021 papers on IF, there is no effect either positive or negative to adhering to IF, so it's essentially pointless. However one interesting paper is a large Ramadan study in Pakistan and China, how IF promoted beneficial gut bacteria by giving your microbiome a few hours break in work so they could propogate themselves.

Conclusion: IF benefits are inconclusive, decide for yourself. Periodic IF like Ramadan may be beneficial to our gut microbiome. I found many warnings too about long IF intervals and excessive calorie restriction that can result in gallstones and other problems in all the survey papers.

5 day periodic fasting

5 or more days of fasting repeated every month or every 3mos+. You cannot do this if diabetic and with other certain conditions you will drop dead unless consulting a doctor. The methods used are either Buchinger method (250 or less calories of soups) or the fasting mimicking diet method (FMD) restricting calories to under 750 for at least 4 days and 1100 for the first day. The 5 day FMD diet is detailed in this book and in the papers released by the USC longevity institute. In brief summary (read the book/papers) the FMD purports in human trials to reduce visceral abdominal fat, promote muscle growth, and in animal models works just as well as chemotherapy to destroy cancer cells by promoting body maintenance which then goes around killing off 'bad cells' while under periodic fasting. In human trials they found FMD is beneficial if you're undergoing chemo.

The average of other periodic fasting studies concluded it increases Christensenella and other beneficial gut bacteria, so again just like the IF Ramadan study giving your stomach a periodic break from breaking down food is probably worth doing.

Conclusion: worth trying the periodic 5 day fasting mimicking diet though you don't have to pay $300 to ProLon the company started by the longevity institute director, their original control group studies are freely available to create your own fasting mimicking diet so long as you respect the macros and don't wing it. The book outlines all the medical conditions that make fasting dangerous without doctor supervision so of course, read that too.

Longevity diet

This is a diet based on research to prevent accelerated aging. The longevity diet for adults is another offspring of the USC longevity institute who gave us the FMD periodic fast. It's detailed in the department director's book (unfortunately also an autobiography) and website (unfortunately filled with marketing). If you don't want to read the book he has plenty of interviews on YouTube about longevity research such as low-protein diets.

The longevity diet is similar to a Pescatarian diet with the following changes:

Macros:

  • 55-60% daily calories from complex-carbohydrates (veggies, grains, pasta and some bread)
  • 30-35% calories from unsaturated fats (3 Tbsp olive oil, nuts, oily fish)
  • 10-11% of calories from proteins (fish/plant protein) of max 0.36g protein per pound of your weight
    • Recommends at least 30g of your daily protein consumed within 1-2hrs of exercise

It considers the minimum recommended daily protein intake of 0.8g/kilo of body weight or 0.36g/pound as the maximum for 65 and under (and more if older) due to various research that excessive protein fuels IGF-1 growth hormone which acts to both accelerate the aging process and possibly grow cancer cells. These recommendations aren't based on a single study, one is this study “Almost everyone is going to have a cancer cell or pre-cancer cell in them at some point. The question is: Does it progress?” Longo said. “Turns out one of the major factors in determining if it does is is protein intake.” The book notes if you are over 65 then IGF-1 levels drop off dramatically and you can increase protein intake to help retain muscle mass in old age.

There is questionable nutrient timing advice of 'must never eat 3-4 hours before sleeping' and other nutrient window type schemes that we already saw don't do anything outside of animal models and the origins of this advice seem to be a single study. He argues you should eat whatever your family bloodline ate, so if you aren't from Peru don't eat stuff like Quinoa or if they never ate Tumeric, then don't eat Tumeric as these ingredients may cause intolerances/inflammation so stick to similar grains or whatever ingredients that your ancestors all ate. The appendix has a fully detailed 2 week diet plan of 1800 calories per day you can adjust to your height/weight, which is almost exclusively dishes from the author's community in Genoa Italy so heavy on pasta and even breakfast cereal, honey, bread etc.

The total abolishment of animal protein and dairy is from their own study at USC, where they claimed that moderate animal protein intake, even within low-protein diets promoted IGF-1 growth more than plant-based protein. As a contradiction a study from March 2021 citing that same meat protein study showed that there is no difference between plant or animal protein when it comes to increasing growth hormones or early death, in both large scale population studies and their own test group of middle aged diabetics: Recent large population-based cohort studies extensively correcting for confounding did not find differences in mortality between vegetarians and non-vegetarians and We conclude that animal and plant protein similarly increase IGF-1 bioavailability while improving metabolic parameters and may be regarded as equivalent in this regard. The no meat or dairy recommendation is also partially based on the author's opinion that people are unfit to determine what moderation of protein intake means and as a whole, eat far too much of all types of protein on average.

The diet recommends taking a fish oil and multivitamin every 3 days as a safety since the author himself is unsure if his plant based diet will result in vitamin and mineral deficiencies when us regular plebs attempt it. Mathematical model for weight loss forecasting

The high fat diet anecdotes above still worked for losing weight, yet they break the energy balance theory (EBT) model, the traditional calories in/calories out model for weight loss. Eating piles of cheese and sausages like Tyson Fury did shouldn't have worked according to the EBT model so something that fell through my decision tree was this paper where they claim a mass balance model has superior forecasting and can explain all the above outlier diets. The paper is short, most of it is citations and consists of solving a differential system like you would any other math model. "The food property that has the capacity to increase body weight is its macronutrient mass and not its potential nutritional energy (calories)". They establish a baseline for 140 days then run a simulation on a 2000 calorie per day model using macros of low carbs, and it shows weight loss. Keeping the exact same energy intake (2k calories) they exchange the macros increasing the carbs and run a simulation which shows weight gain, yet the EBT model claims this can't happen because it only models energy.

Summary: "Implicit in the EBT discourse is that body fat accumulates only if fat intake exceeds fat oxidation, even under dietary energy restriction, which explains why this theory argues in favor of low-fat-diets as the optimal fat loss dietary treatment".

Conclusion: Adjust macros to gain or lose weight, ignore the energy balance model.

HIIT vs SIT vs MICT

HIIT

Defined as high-intensity interval training, with no universal standard on session duration it consists of moderately intensive continuous training (MICT) mixed with intervals of repeated short to long bouts of intensity followed by rest periods. Going through the 2021 papers, a systematic review of previous research found that HIIT resulted in a significant boost in testosterone and cortisol levels immediately after a single 30m session, which disappeared after 24 hours back to baseline levels meaning you should only ever do it in moderation unless you want your immune system to stop working and die from a heart attack due to stress being flooded into your bloodstream. 60m sessions produced a greater increase in cortisol. The same review states there wasn't enough controlled studies in the papers they reviewed and had limited data so a real controlled study of HIIT on the body's hormone system needs to be done in the future.

Scraping for other papers that tout HIIT benefits, the average I found was HIIT done for 15-30m for as little as 6 weeks, done 3 days a week, preserves VO2 peak for up to 4 years in inactive older adults, meaning even if you stop you retain your peak oxygen uptake. It also was shown to improve 8 indicators of cardiometabolic health compared to 3 for steady state cardio such as blood pressure and total cholesterol. For fat loss on average MICT burns visceral fat and helps you lean out just the same as HIIT does, it's inconclusive whether HIIT is any better for fat burning as you get one study that claims it's great, and another that says there's no difference, and it repeats like this. HIIT 3x a week was also found on average to have reduced impulsivity, and improve short-term memory in a bunch of HIIT psychology studies I found.

There's some entertaining papers of surveying typical young men who binge drink beer if it affected their HIIT benefits, which turned out to be no, alcohol is fine to consume and receive the benefits of HIIT.

Conclusion: HIIT has many benefits over MICT but none of them are fat loss as it's inconclusive if one is any better than the other for reducing visceral/body fat using the EBT model of calories in/calories out weight loss. It should be done in moderation or else you may get flooded with stress hormones which take 24 hours to leave your body after a single session. There doesn't seem to be any benefit of doing more than 30 minutes of HIIT per session.

SIT

Here is a 2015 documentary about sprint interval training. In a survey of 2021 papers on SIT the methods were (on a bike) 2 min warmup at normal pace, 3 x 20 second (sometimes 30 second) 'all out' intensity intervals interspersed with 2 mins of recovery normal pace cycling, then a 3 min cool down at normal pace. In total 12.5m or so of cardio done in 28 sessions over 12 weeks resulted in 21% increase in V02 peak oxygen uptake and 6% increase in peak cardio output. 'All out intensity' is defined as pedaling or running like your life depends on it.

If you read the 2021 papers, SIT also reduced EGF or epidermal growth factor in older men to the same levels as the younger men in the study, lower EGF is associated with longevity and healthy aging. However, in a systematic review of all previous studies to date (2021), they found all the SIT studies except for 2 were so low quality they weren't even worth the paper they were printed on. If you're wondering how they review, they use the Cochrane handbook. As an anecdote SE asian fighting clubs have long used sprint interval training to boost the cardio of their fighters, you go out in the parking lot or alley and sprint from one side to the other in intervals which takes about 20 seconds.

Conclusion: it's inconclusive if SIT is superior to HIIT or MICT because many of the previous studies were junk tier quality but since I own an exercise bike anyway, and it only takes 12.5 minutes, I may as well do it 3 days a week and be my own test subject.

Tristar coach Firhas Zahabi recommends consistency over intensity if doing bodyweight exercises like push-ups which are technical. Intensity he claims should be periodic. Start at your max reps to establish a baseline, divide by two, then everyday add more reps incrementally so you aren't too sore to do these bodyweight exercises everyday. Conclusion: injecting periodic intensity into a workout has benefits but consistency with daily moderate workouts leads to better improvements with less injuries over time for sports training and bodyweight technical exercises.

The Strategy

We took a tour of fitness memes and papers. We learned that the EBT model of calories in/calories out is flawed and adjusting macros work better for forecasting weight loss. We learned that reducing saturated fat and excess protein will help us avoid early death, and at least 30g protein should be consumed after exercise. We learned HIIT done for 15 to 30 minutes, 2-3 days a week, or possibly SIT done 12.5 minutes, 3 days a week, gives you the same cardio benefits of doing 1hr sessions of moderately intensive cardio done at a persistent pace. We learned we should do strength training consistently and mix in periodic intensity. We learned we should do 2.5-5hrs weekly of cardio but it was noted all the benefits are in the first 2.5 hours you do during the week, with the rest optional.

Finally we learned we should plan out every workout, analyzing the results to constantly adjust our plan and make it better.
