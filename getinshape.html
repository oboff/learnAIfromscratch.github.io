State of the Art

What's the 'state of the art' in weight loss/fitness? I'm an everyday pleb who wanted to return to being in shape after a covid lockdown break of gaining weight, so I will go through a very casual tour of some fitness and obesity research, mix in some anecdotes, all content that I've found with the help of a basic decision tree algorithm filtering a scrape of primarily 2021 research data to seperate the signal from the noise, then take the average of whatever I find to avoid all the marketing hype and ideology in nutrition and fitness. At the end I will put together a program and try it, see what happens.

Consider this merely a Medium-tier poor quality blog post but with 90% less filler and advertising, and I will sometimes point to a single study then in the same paragraph discredit single studies so take with grain of salt. Spelling mistakes and poor grammar/editing will be prevalent because I had 1.5 weeks to write this up in my spare time which is not so spare.

Heavy metals, pesticides

I was surprised to learn that there is varying levels of arsenic, lead, mercury, cadmium and of course pesticides in spinach, carrots, all fruit, basically anything you eat has some kind of heavy metals in it and if it's a plant likely pesticides too. Taking the average of all recent papers, any leafy greens in particular spinach had one of the highest levels of arsenic, cadmium and lead. Fish that consume other fish had the highest levels of heavy metals where sardines had less heavy metals due to them being on the lower end of the ocean food chain. A few 2021 and older studies found that washing leafy green vegetables with basified water with sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) removed almost all of the pesticides and significantly decreased the metals leading them to conjecture that heavy metals contamination was mainly 'foliar deposition' which I assume means aerosol surface contamination of the leaves ie: you can wash it off. As a contradiction microbiome researchers advocate to wash food less so you get increased beneficial bacteria in your gut.

I didn't have the time to categorize, sort and average pet food contamination but my scrape found many recent pet food testing studies on heavy metals content in dry pelleted food either plant or meat based, many countries have no specific legislation guaranteeing the content.

Because the ocean is filled with plastic trash, let's take a tour of some of the microplastics studies of small fish like sardines and take the average of what we find.

An August 2020 microplastics in sardines study by a Queensland U researcher who in their methods describes going to a local Australian market and buying fresh seafood, claimed sardines have the highest concentrations of micro plastics. However a month earlier, a Western Australia U study that only analyzed local sardine populations found they contained relatively low concentrations of plastic so completely contradictory. So the average of 'very bad' to 'hardly any' is maybe there's a threat level of microplastics and fine for me, I'll just keep eating Atlantic ocean sardines moderately every 3 days or so to get essential fatty oils and calcium.

In other research, on average microplastics have been found everywhere including freshwater samples in the highest elevation lake in North America leading to the conjecture that microplastic dust exists in our atmosphere and contaminates everything but this was described as 'potentially a problem', where microplastics in seafood had an effect on their health it is still inconclusive it's effect on humans who pass them when consumed however the primary concern of breathing and injesting microplastics is the fact they pick up nasty toxins in our environment that cling to them, not the injestion of microplastics themselves yet anyway.

There only exists a single 2020 study I could find of estimates of global average microplastic consumption, which turned out to be 0.1–5g of microplastics weekly (a huge variance, 0.1g vs 5g) through various exposure pathways like drinking water, breathing the air, food, if you want to read it paste the DOI into sci-hub (domain changes all the time as governments and publisher lawyers go after sci-hub) but keep in mind, they took averages of previous papers but did not do systematic review on their methods, and we will see soon that systematic review often discredits entire fields of research as a waste of paper (the studies on SIT cardio) because their methods are filled with bias and even sometimes rely on unmonitored compliance or self-surveys. For example the 'microplastics in honey' study that spawned dozens of media articles is discredited by another study but since this whole guide is to merely take the average, it's probably safe to assume a dust of microplastics covers everything and you're eating and breathing them daily.

Conclusion: If you are worried about microplastics you can buy tap filters that claim to '99% remove all microplastics' (obviously verify this marketing). If you are worried about microplastic dust, heavy metals and pesticides on food you can mix baking soda into the water you use wash them, if you are are worried about injesting microplastics by eating fish/seafood then don't eat it, buy fish oil supplements instead but how do you know the supplement itself isn't filled with microplastics and heavy metals?

Intermittent fasting

Intermittent fasting or IF is a catch all term for numerous different fasting protocols such as feeding time windows like only consume food during a 8-12hr period in 24hrs, only eat during sunlight (circadian rhythm diet) or 5:2 diet, 5 days feeding and 2 days fasting type schemes. In animal models there is many touted benefits but does this translate to humans?

These guys analyzed all non-animal model time restricted eating studies from 2000 until 2019. Result: inconclusive, and with the highest dropout rates of participants meaning odds are if you try this yourself, you're going to quit. "Intermittent fasting has gained much attention as a popular weight-loss strategy over the last few years. Controlled studies demonstrated that IF (which can have very different protocols) is a safe alternative to daily caloric restriction, with similar weight loss results. Nonetheless, its effects on weight are directly related to caloric restriction and not the metabolic effects of fasting". In other words, under the calories in/calories out model of weight maintenance, IF may help with eating less calories since you can't graze on food all day and have to observe a window of eating time but all it's beneficial effects in animals do not manifest in humans.

Taking the average of all 2021 papers on IF, there is no effect either positive or negative to adhering to IF, so it's essentially pointless. However one interesting paper is a large Ramadan study in Pakistan and China, how IF promoted beneficial gut bacteria by giving your microbiome a few hours break in work so they could propogate themselves.

Conclusion: IF benefits are inconclusive, though periodic IF like Ramadan may be beneficial to our gut microbiome. I found many warnings too about long IF intervals and excessive calorie restriction that can result in gallstones and other problems in all the survey papers.

Periodic fasting

This is defined as recurring fasting, specifically 5 or more days of fasting repeated every month or every 3mos+. You cannot do this if diabetic and with other certain conditions you will drop dead unless consulting a doctor. The methods used are either Buchinger method (250 or less calories of soups) or the fasting mimicking diet method (FMD) restricting calories to under 750 for at least 4 days and 1100 for the first day. The 5 day FMD diet is detailed in this book and in the papers released by the USC longevity institute. In brief summary (read the book/papers) the FMD purports in human trials to reduce visceral abdominal fat, promote muscle growth, and in animal models works just as well as chemotherapy to destroy cancer cells by promoting body maintenance which then goes around killing off 'bad cells' while under periodic fasting. They found FMD is beneficial if you're undergoing chemo.

The average of other periodic fasting studies concluded it increases Christensenella and other beneficial gut bacteria, so again just like the IF Ramadan study giving your stomach a periodic break from breaking down food is probably worth doing.

Conclusion: worth trying the periodic 5 day fasting mimicking diet though you don't have to pay $300 to ProLon the company started by the longevity institute director, their original control group studies are freely available to create your own fasting mimicking diet. The book outlines all the medical conditions that make fasting dangerous without doctor supervision so of course, read that too.

Unfortunately it is filled with anecdotes, pointing to a single study for much of it's claims (except the fasting research, which should be removed from this book and put into a standalone book). Mathematical model for weight loss forecasting

The high fat diet anecdotes above still worked for losing weight, yet they break the energy balance theory (EBT) model, the traditional calories in/calories out model for weight loss. Eating piles of cheese and sausages like Tyson Fury did shouldn't have worked according to the EBT model so something that fell through my decision tree was this paper where they claim a mass balance model has superior forecasting and can explain all the above outlier diets. The paper is short, most of it is citations and consists of solving a differential system like you would any other math model. "The food property that has the capacity to increase body weight is its macronutrient mass and not its potential nutritional energy (calories)". They establish a baseline for 140 days then run a simulation on a 2000 calorie per day model using macros of low carbs, and it shows weight loss. Keeping the exact same energy intake (2k calories) they exchange the macros increasing the carbs and run a simulation which shows weight gain, yet the EBT model claims this can't happen because it only models energy.

Summary: "Implicit in the EBT discourse is that body fat accumulates only if fat intake exceeds fat oxidation, even under dietary energy restriction, which explains why this theory argues in favor of low-fat-diets as the optimal fat loss dietary treatment".

Conclusion: Adjust macros to gain or lose weight, ignore the energy balance model.

HIIT vs SIT vs MICT

HIIT

Defined as high-intensity interval training, with no universal standard on session duration it consists of moderately intensive continuous training (MICT) mixed with intervals of repeated short to long bouts of intensity followed by rest periods. Going through the 2021 papers, a systematic review of previous research found that HIIT resulted in a significant boost in testosterone and cortisol levels immediately after a single 30m session, which disappeared after 24 hours back to baseline levels meaning you should only ever do it in moderation unless you want your immune system to stop working and die from a heart attack due to stress being flooded into your bloodstream. 60m sessions produced a greater increase in cortisol. The same review states there wasn't enough controlled studies in the papers they reviewed and had limited data so a real controlled study of HIIT on the body's hormone system needs to be done in the future.

Scraping for other papers that tout HIIT benefits, the average I found was HIIT done for 15-30m for as little as 6 weeks, done 3 days a week, preserves VO2 peak for up to 4 years in inactive older adults, meaning even if you stop you retain your peak oxygen uptake. It also was shown to improve 8 indicators of cardiometabolic health compared to 3 for steady state cardio such as blood pressure and total cholesterol. For fat loss on average MICT burns visceral fat and helps you lean out just the same as HIIT does, it's inconclusive whether HIIT is any better for fat burning as you get one study that claims it's great, and another that says there's no difference, and it repeats like this. HIIT 3x a week was also found on average to have reduced impulsivity, and improve short-term memory in a bunch of HIIT psychology studies I found.

There's some entertaining papers of surveying young men who drink beer if it affected their HIIT benefits, which turned out to be no, alcohol is fine to consume and receive the benefits of HIIT.

Conclusion: HIIT has a ton of benefits over MICT but none of them are fat loss as it's inconclusive if one is any better than the other for reducing visceral/body fat. It should be done in moderation or else you may get flooded with stress hormones which take 24 hours to leave your body after a single session. There doesn't seem to be any benefit of doing more than 30 minutes of HIIT per session.

SIT

Here is a 2015 documentary about sprint interval training. In a survey of 2021 papers on SIT the methods were (on a bike) 2 min warmup at normal pace, 3 x 20 second (sometimes 30 second) 'all out' intensity intervals interspersed with 2 mins of recovery normal pace cycling, then a 3 min cool down at normal pace. In total 12.5m or so of cardio done in 28 sessions over 12 weeks resulted in 21% increase in V02 peak oxygen uptake and 6% increase in peak cardio output. 'All out intensity' is defined as pedaling or running like your life depends on it.

If you read the 2021 papers, SIT also reduced EGF or epidermal growth factor in older men to the same levels as the younger men in the study, lower EGF is associated with longevity and healthy aging. However, in a systematic review of all previous studies to date (2021), they found all the SIT studies except for 2 were so low quality they weren't even worth the paper they were printed on. If you're wondering how they review, they use the Cochrane handbook. As an anecdote SE asian fighting clubs have long used sprint interval training to boost the cardio of their fighters, you go out in the parking lot or alley and sprint from one side to the other in intervals which takes about 20 seconds.

Conclusion: it's inconclusive if SIT is superior to HIIT or even MICT because many of the previous studies were junk tier quality but since I own an exercise bike anyway, and it only takes 12.5 minutes, I may as well do it 3 days a week and be my own test subject.

The Strategy

We took a tour of fitness memes and papers. We learned that the EBT model of calories in/calories out is flawed and adjusting macros work better for forecasting weight loss. We learned that reducing saturated fat and excess protein will help us avoid early death. We learned HIIT done for 15 to 30m 2-3 days a week, or possibly SIT done 12.5 minutes 3 days a week, gives you the same cardio benefits of doing 1hr of moderately intensive cardio done at a persistent pace. We learned we should do strength training consistently and mix in periodic intensity. We learned we should do 2.5-5hrs weekly of cardio but it was noted all the benefits are in the first 2.5 hours you do during the week, with the rest optional.

Finally we learned we should plan out every workout, analyzing the results to constantly adjust our plan and make it better.

Calculate 0.31-36 protein grams per your bodyweight in pounds to be consumed tomorrow if under the age of 65 and schedule the bulk of this protein, at least 30g to be consumed 1-2 hours after your strength workout if you're doing one. Whenever you recover, plan the next workout by dividing the reps in half so you can do this everyday. If you could only do one properly executed hindu pushup on the first day, the next workout do it as controlled and slow as possible, which will be harder. Then add in more reps incrementally.

Plan your cardio & strength conditioning

I'm going to try coach Firhas Zahabi's advice of shadow boxing @5:01 to build cardio and gain a self-defense skill at the same time, this seems more interesting and motivating than running laps everyday. His advice if you don't have any equipment (paraphrasing): "Do calisthenics for 1 minute, shadow box for 1 minute, repeat that for 15-20 minutes it will be a killer workout to get you really lean".

I picked Muay Boran which is 'old style Muay Thai' meaning street fighting before it became a sport focused style, but you can pick from dozens of boxing styles to shadow practice and mix with Filipino dirty boxing, stick fighting, Jeet Kune Do whatever personally interests you so you have motivation to practice it.

First cardio workout

Plan a MICT or moderately intensive cardio training routine based on the above shadow forms, meaning you have your heart rate going 70% of max, meaning moderate intervals of bodyweight exercises or jogging in place, some burpees if you can do them, no crazy intense intervals yet until whenever you think you've built up some minimum fitness from zero to maybe can survive a HIIT routine.

When you want to plan a HIIT session it would look like this or the entire Tiger Muay Thai conditioning notice the style of both, 30-60s of extreme intensity, rest, do some MICT, rest, go back to high intensity, repeat. That Muay Thai strength conditioning workout they do 10 exercises, 15 seconds rest after each one, when finished round 1 wait 2 minutes and repeat again for a total of 3 rounds.

This guy's channel has full 60m workouts from warmup to cooldown with shadow boxing and HIIT intervals to use as a template or just follow exactly what he does, the length you do it for is up to you remember weekly exercise only needs to be 2.5 hours of primarily MICT for basic heart health with one of those a HIIT session if you're going to bare minimum and get most of the benefits.

Street defense

Reminder this is only for keeping fitness interesting by building up a skill, hardly any of these moves are safe to use in a street fight without heavy bag conditioning all your limbs or at least aqua bag conditioning your hands and elbows. Throwing a round kick in a street fight without conditioning on a heavy bag is a guaranteed way to screw up your shin, sprain ankle if you miss the target because you don't practice against a moving bag, or break your foot. Throwing a high teep push kick you can blow out your groin or they could catch it and you're screwed. For self defense, you can use risk the vertical bareknuckle jab, push kick only below the waist, Lerdrit knee, headbutt and clothesline or back of the ear palm strike, maybe some of the elbows if you must in the street.

Because you now carry yourself differently in public as you have increased confidence, it is less likely that somebody will single you out for an assault, and even more unlikely if you always show respect to everybody you encounter at all times, then nobody has to check your bad behavior in the street leading to a preventable fight. The above information was collected from bareknuckle boxing videos, Bas Rutten recommendations from his days a nightclub bouncer, and Firhas Zahabi if you want to watch them yourself but it's also just common sense.
